Exploring a Clinical Trial Dataset

Session 2: Hypothesis testing

1. Where we left off

In Session 1 we found that the High Dose group had a lower average post-treatment blood pressure than the Placebo group.

However, one important question remains.

Is this difference large enough that we can attribute it to the treatment, or could it simply have occurred by chance?

To answer this question, we need a statistical test.

2. Why do we need a hypothesis test?

Although the High Dose group had a lower average post-treatment blood pressure than the Placebo group, averages calculated from a sample are rarely exactly the same as the averages in the whole population.

A hypothesis test helps us decide whether the difference we observed is likely to reflect a real treatment effect or whether it could simply be due to random sampling variation.

3. Choosing the right statistical test

Researchers do not choose statistical tests at random.

The choice depends on the research question and the type of data being analysed.

Think like a researcher

Our research question is:

Do patients receiving the High Dose treatment have lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

Ask yourself:

  • What is our outcome variable?
  • Is it numerical or categorical?
  • How many groups are we comparing?
Solution
Variable Description Type Role
sbp_post SBP after treatment Numeric Dependent variable (DV)
group Treatment group (Placebo / High Dose) Categorical (2 levels) Independent variable (IV)

Therefore, we need a statistical test that compares the means of two groups.

What is an independent variable?

It’s the thing you’re grouping by or comparing across — in this case, treatment group.

Summary Table of different test statistics for hypothesis testing
Research question Variables Test
Compare two group means One numeric + one categorical (2 groups) t-test
Compare three or more group means One numeric + one categorical (3+ groups) ANOVA
Compare two categorical variables Two categorical Chi-square
Relationship between two numeric variables Two numeric Correlation

The independent samples t-test

The independent samples t-test compares the average value of a numerical variable between two independent groups.

In our study:

  • numerical variable: sbp_post
  • groups: High Dose and Placebo

This is exactly the type of research question that a t-test is designed to answer.

Example: Is systolic blood pressure (SBP) different for patients who took High Dose Hypotensol vs those who took Placebo?

4. Writing the hypotheses

  • Every hypothesis test begins with a research question.

  • The hypotheses are derived directly from that research question.

Tip
  • The research question tells us what we want to investigate.
  • The hypotheses translate that question into statements that we can test statistically.

Our research question is:

Do patients receiving the High Dose treatment have lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

From this research question, we can formulate two hypotheses.

  1. The null hypothesis says there is no difference between the groups.

  2. The alternative hypothesis says there is a difference, or that the difference goes in the direction predicted by the research question.

Hypotheses for our research question

Null hypothesis (H₀):
The High Dose and Placebo groups have the same average post-treatment systolic blood pressure.

Alternative hypothesis (H₁):
The High Dose group has lower average post-treatment systolic blood pressure than the Placebo group.

Why a one-sided t-test?

There are two common types of independent samples t-test.

1. One-sided t-test

  • A one-sided t-test is used when the research question predicts the direction of the difference.

For example:

Do patients receiving the High Dose treatment have lower post-treatment blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

Here, we are specifically predicting that the High Dose group will have lower blood pressure.

2. Two-sided t-test

  • A two-sided t-test is used when we only want to know whether the groups are different, without predicting which group will have the higher or lower values.

For example:

Is there a difference in post-treatment blood pressure between the High Dose and Placebo groups?

This question does not predict the direction of the difference.

Which one are we using?

Because our research question predicts that the High Dose group will have lower blood pressure than the Placebo group, we use a one-sided t-test.

5. Running the t-test in R

Preparing the data

  • R compares the groups in the order they are stored.

  • To match our research hypothesis, we want R to compare:

High Dose < Placebo

The following code sets the groups in this order.

So we set High Dose as the first group and Placebo as the second group.

Code 
bp_data <- bp_data |>
  mutate(
    group = factor(
      group,
      levels = c("High Dose", "Placebo")
    )
  )

Running the test

Test whether the High Dose group has a lower mean sbp_post than the Placebo group.

Code 
t.test(
  sbp_post ~ group,
  data = bp_data,
  alternative = "less"
)

    Welch Two Sample t-test

data:  sbp_post by group
t = -7.3645, df = 57.443, p-value = 3.767e-10
alternative hypothesis: true difference in means between group High Dose and group Placebo is less than 0
95 percent confidence interval:
      -Inf -13.92153
sample estimates:
mean in group High Dose   mean in group Placebo 
                 125.12                  143.13

Understanding the code

  • sbp_post ~ group tells R to compare post-treatment blood pressure across the treatment groups.

  • data = bp_data tells R to use the filtered dataset containing only High Dose and Placebo patients.

  • alternative = "less" tells R that our hypothesis is directional: we expect the High Dose group to have lower blood pressure than the Placebo group.

Optional: Understanding the ~ symbol

In R, the symbol ~ can be read as “by” or “grouped by.”

For example:

sbp_post ~ group

can be read as:

Compare post-treatment systolic blood pressure by treatment group.

6. Interpreting the t-test output

Running t.test() produces several pieces of information.

Before reading the explanation below, see if you can identify the following in the output:

  • the group means

  • the t statistic;

  • the degrees of freedom (df)

  • the p-value; and

  • the confidence interval.

Code 
t.test(sbp_post ~ group,
  data = bp_data,
  alternative = "less")

    Welch Two Sample t-test

data:  sbp_post by group
t = -7.3645, df = 57.443, p-value = 3.767e-10
alternative hypothesis: true difference in means between group High Dose and group Placebo is less than 0
95 percent confidence interval:
      -Inf -13.92153
sample estimates:
mean in group High Dose   mean in group Placebo 
                 125.12                  143.13

Where can you find each value?

Output What does it tell us?
t The t-statistic
df The degrees of freedom
p-value Whether the difference is statistically significant
95 percent confidence interval A range of plausible values for the true difference between the group means
mean in group High Dose The average SBP for the High Dose group

Step 1: Compare the group means

The output reports the mean post-treatment blood pressure for each group.

  • mean in group High Dose 125.12

  • mean in group Placebo 143.13

Question

Which treatment group has the lower average blood pressure?

Answer

The High Dose group has the lower average post-treatment blood pressure.

This agrees with the descriptive statistics we calculated in Session 1.

Step 2: Look at the p-value

The p-value helps us decide whether the observed difference is likely to reflect a real treatment effect or whether it could simply be due to chance.

What is a p-value?

The p-value tells us how compatible our data are with the idea that there is no real difference between the treatment groups.

A small p-value suggests that the observed difference is unlikely to be explained by chance alone.

The p-value from our analysis is:

3.767 × 10⁻¹⁰

This is much smaller than 0.05.

Decision rule

  • If p < 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis.

  • If p ≥ 0.05, we do not reject the null hypothesis.

Step 3: What about the other numbers?

  • The t-test output also reports a t-statistic, degrees of freedom (df) and a confidence interval.

  • These values are important because they are used to calculate and describe the statistical result.

  • For today’s workshop, the most important value to interpret is the p-value, since it tells us whether the observed difference is statistically significant.

Optional: What do these values mean?

  • t-statistic – measures how large the difference between the group means is relative to the variation in the data.

  • Degrees of freedom (df) – a value used when calculating the t-test.

  • Confidence interval – gives a range of plausible values for the true difference between the group means. Because we performed a one-sided t-test, R reports a one-sided confidence interval.

7. Conclusion

The t-test produced a very small p-value (3.77 × 10⁻¹⁰), which is much smaller than 0.05.

Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis.

The results provide strong evidence that patients receiving the High Dose Hypotensol treatment had lower average post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment.

Reporting results

Researchers usually summarise their findings in a single sentence.

For example:

An independent samples t-test found that patients in the High Dose group (\(M = 125.12\)) had significantly lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than those in the Placebo group (\(M = 143.13\)), \(t(57.44) = −7.36, p < .001\)

Understanding the reporting format

Research papers often report:

  • \(M\) = mean (average)

  • \(SD\) = standard deviation

  • \(t(df)\) = the t-statistic and its degrees of freedom

  • \(p\) = the p-value

This provides a concise summary of both the descriptive statistics (the group averages) and the inferential statistics (the hypothesis test).

8. Key takeaway

In Session 1, we used descriptive statistics to describe the data.

In Session 2, we used inferential statistics to decide whether the observed difference between the treatment groups was statistically significant.

Together, these steps form a typical workflow for analysing data in a clinical trial.
