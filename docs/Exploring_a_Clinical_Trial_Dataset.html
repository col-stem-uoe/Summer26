Exploring a Clinical Trial Dataset

In this activity, you will use R to explore data from a fictional clinical trial investigating a new blood pressure medication called Hypotensol. You will answer a research question by identifying the relevant variables, summarising the data, and creating simple visualisations.

Background

High blood pressure is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease. Researchers have developed a new medication called Hypotensol and want to determine whether it reduces blood pressure more effectively than a placebo.

To investigate this, they conducted a randomised clinical trial.

Research question

Do patients receiving the High Dose Hypotensol treatment have lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

Dataset

To answer this question, we will analyse data from a fictional clinical trial involving 90 patients with high blood pressure.

Participants were randomly assigned to one of three treatment groups:

  1. Placebo
  2. Low Dose Hypotensol (25 mg)
  3. High Dose Hypotensol (50 mg)

Patients were recruited from:

  • NHS Lothian

  • NHS Greater Glasgow

Variables in the Dataset

Variable Name Description
id Patient ID number (anonymised unique identifier)
group Treatment group (Placebo / Low Dose / High Dose)
sbp_pre Systolic blood pressure before treatment (mmHg)
sbp_post Systolic blood pressure after treatment (mmHg)
improved Did the patient improve? (Yes / No)
age Age of patient in years
sex Sex of patient (Male / Female)
weight_kg Weight at baseline in kilograms
bmi Body Mass Index (kg/m²)
drug Drug administered (Hypotensol / None)
dose_mg Dose of drug administered in milligrams
post_check_weeks Weeks after treatment when post-treatment BP was measured
home_sbp_avg Average home blood pressure reading after treatment (mmHg)
followup_1y 1-year follow-up status (Completed / Missed)
clinic NHS clinic where the patient was treated

Researcher’s mindset

Every statistical analysis begins with a research question. The research question determines:

  • which variables we need;

  • whether those variables are categorical or numerical; and

  • which statistical methods are appropriate.

Learning objectives

  • identify categorical and numerical variables;

  • use R to summarise and visualise data;

  • interpret descriptive statistics in the context of a research question.

Step 4: How many patients are in each treatment group?

Count how many patients are in each treatment group.

The functioncount()

  • counts how many observations belong to each category of the variable group.

  • mainly used with categorical variables.

In our dataset, the variable group contains the treatment assigned to each patient:

  • Placebo

  • Low Dose

  • High Dose

The syntax

count(dataset, variable)

where:

  • dataset = the dataset we want to analyse;

  • variable = the categorical variable we want to count.

In our example

count(data, group)
# A tibble: 3 × 2
  group         n
  <chr>     <int>
1 High Dose    30
2 Low Dose     30
3 Placebo      30

  • count() = count the number of observations

  • data = the dataset we want to analyse

  • group = the variable we want to count

  • n = shows the number of patients in each group

Interpreting the output: Why is this useful?

The output shows the number of patients in each treatment group.

Before analysing data, researchers often ask

  • How many patients are in each treatment group?
  • Are the treatment groups balanced?
  • Are any treatment groups missing?

In this clinical trial, each treatment group contains **30 patients**, so the groups are perfectly balanced.

Step 5: Create a new dataset (subset) for our research question

Let’s return to our research question:

Do patients receiving the High Dose Hypotensol treatment have lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

Our dataset contains three treatment groups:

  • Placebo

  • Low Dose

  • High Dose

However, our research question only compares High Dose and Placebo.

Therefore, before analysing the data, we will create a new dataset containing only the patients relevant to our research question.

The filter() function

The filter() function selects only the rows that satisfy a condition.

The syntax

filter(variable == value)

or, when keeping multiple values,

filter(variable %in% c(value1, value2))

In our example

The filter() function allows us to select observations that meet specific conditions.

bp_data <- data |>
  filter(group %in% c("Placebo", "High Dose"))

We can read this code as: Take the dataset data, keep only the patients in the Placebo and High Dose groups, and store the result in a new dataset called bp_data.

Reading the code

Let’s read the code from left to right.

  • data = start with the original dataset.

  • |> = pass the dataset to the next function.

  • filter() = keep only the rows that satisfy our condition.

  • group %in% c("Placebo", "High Dose") = keep only patients in the Placebo or High Dose treatment groups.

  • bp_data <- = store the filtered dataset in a new object called bp_data.

The Assignment Operator: <-

  • The symbol <- means: Store the result on the right-hand side in the object on the left-hand side.

  • In our analysis : bp_data <- data means ” Create a new object called bp_data and store the dataset data inside it.”

The Pipe Operator: |>

  • The symbol |> is called the pipe operator.

  • it means: Take the result from the left and pass it to the function on the right.

 data |>
    count(group)
# A tibble: 3 × 2
  group         n
  <chr>     <int>
1 High Dose    30
2 Low Dose     30
3 Placebo      30

can be read as:

Take the dataset data and count the number of observations in each treatment group.

Why is this useful?

  • Researchers rarely analyse every observation in a dataset.

  • Instead, they select the subset of data that answers their research question.

  • In this example, the Low Dose group is not relevant because our research question compares only the High Dose and Placebo groups.

Step 6: Comparing Treatment Groups

Let’s return to our research question:

Do patients receiving the High Dose Hypotensol treatment have lower post-treatment systolic blood pressure than patients receiving the Placebo treatment?

To answer this question, we are comparing:

  • a numerical variable (sbp_post), which records each patient’s post-treatment systolic blood pressure.

In the previous step, we created a new dataset called bp_data, which contains only the High Dose and Placebo groups. These are the only patients relevant to our research question.
